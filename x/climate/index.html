Climate Change: A Rational Perspective

Evidence-Based Analysis Over Fear-Driven Narratives

The Problem with Climate Discourse

Climate change is real and measurable. However, public discourse often conflates scientific facts with political advocacy, fear-based narratives with data-driven analysis, and short-term activism with long-term solutions.

Our Approach
We separate measurable facts from predictions, acknowledge what science actually tells us, and evaluate policy responses based on cost-effectiveness rather than emotional appeals.

Key Distinctions

Facts Are Facts
Climate change is measurable through temperature records, sea levels, and atmospheric CO₂. These are observations, not opinions.

Models Are Predictions
Climate models are valuable tools validated against historical data, but they project scenarios, not certainties.

Policy Is Political
How we respond to climate change involves trade-offs in economics, equity, and priorities—inherently political decisions.

Efficiency Matters
Not all climate actions are equally effective. Some deliver $11 in benefits per dollar spent, others just $0.11.

The Measurable Facts

What We Know With Certainty

  • Global Temperature: Earth has warmed ~1.2 °C since pre-industrial times (direct measurements)
  • Sea Level Rise: ~25 cm since 1880; currently ~4 mm/year (satellite data)
  • CO₂ Levels: ~425 ppm, up from ~280 ppm pre-industrial
  • Ice Loss: Arctic sea ice declining ~12% per decade

Global Welfare Context

Global Welfare Trajectory Despite Climate Change

Projected GDP per capita shows massive growth by 2100. Climate damages reduce growth from ~450% to ~434% of 2020 levels—significant, but manageable within overall prosperity gains.

Source: Lomborg (2020), based on IPCC SSP scenarios

What This Actually Means

Climate change is happening and is primarily driven by human activity (~76% attribution). However, impacts are moderate when viewed in the context of long-term human development.

“Untreated climate change equates to a 3.6% GDP reduction by 2100—significant but dwarfed by 434% net welfare growth.”
— Bjorn Lomborg, Welfare in the 21st Century (2020)

Additional observations:

  • Climate-related death risk has dropped 99% over the last century
  • Economic costs of extreme weather declined 26% since 1990
  • No detected global trend in hurricane frequency (IPCC)
  • Global wildfire burned area declined 25% since 1998

Three Key Questions

1. Is Climate Change Happening?

Yes. This is a fact.
Independent measurements confirm warming, sea-level rise, and atmospheric changes.

2. Why Is It Happening?

Primarily human CO₂ emissions.

  • Human contribution: ~76%
  • Natural variability: ~24%
  • Current warming rate: ~10× faster than post-ice-age recoveries

Natural cycles exist, but they explain only a small portion of observed warming since 1950.

3. What Should We Do About It?

This is where science ends and politics begins.

Science informs risks and options, but policy choices depend on values, trade-offs, and priorities. The key question becomes:

What delivers the most benefit per dollar spent?

Why Panic-Driven Policies Fail

Fear-based decision-making often leads to:

  • Unrealistic temperature targets costing trillions more than benefits
  • Underinvestment in adaptation (one of the most effective strategies)
  • Resource diversion from poverty, health, and education
  • Symbolic commitments with minimal real-world impact

“Sensible environmental progress has come not from alarmism but from innovation, growth, and smart policy.”
— Bjorn Lomborg (2025)

Top 10 Effective Actions

Evidence-based strategies that maximize impact per dollar invested.

Global Level

  1. Green R&D Investment
    $100B/year into clean energy and carbon capture
    Impact: ~$11 benefit per dollar

  2. International Carbon Pricing
    Moderate, rising price (~$50/ton by mid-century)

  3. Halt Deforestation
    Protect forests and expand reforestation
    Impact: ~10 GtCO₂/year

  4. Climate Finance for Developing Nations
    $100–150B/year for adaptation with strong social returns

National Level

  1. Moderate Carbon Taxes
    Revenue-neutral, realistic pricing
    Benefit-cost ratio ~2:1

  2. Renewable Energy Transition
    Grid modernization and efficiency standards

  3. Resilient Infrastructure
    Flood defenses, water systems, climate-smart agriculture
    Impact: 99% reduction in death risk

Personal Level

  1. Energy Efficiency
    Insulation, LEDs, EVs, public transport

  2. Dietary Shifts
    Reduce meat consumption and food waste
    Potential: ~8 GtCO₂/year globally

  3. Evidence-Based Advocacy
    Support policies focused on R&D and adaptation
